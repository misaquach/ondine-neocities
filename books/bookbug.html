My thoughts

Notes from Underground (Dostoyevsky)

I liked the author’s depiction of everyday people’s secret inner problems and discontentment, and the metaphors she uses to describe emotions. Her interest in psychology reminds me of Dostoyevsky, but she’s more poetic, as well as practical — she seems to express that one doesn’t need mental breakdowns and tumultuous life events to be unhappy in life; the people you see every day, the things you read in the papers, and the things expected from you by society may be enough to make life weary, uncomfortable, and colorless. Because that is the “theme”, there isn’t a strong sense of plot in the book, it mainly describes its characters’ lives.

Stylistically, I found her difficult to read. Her sentences are winding and she’s merciless with commas; reading the novel was a sluggish process. Maybe I’m just not used to Victorian locution, but I ultimately did not finish the novel.

Also, Elilenti’s review of this book has a very good analysis!

To The Lighthouse (Woolfe)

To be frank, I don’t think this deserves the acclaim it gets compared to his other works, like The Idiot — its themes are more simple and layerless than usual for Dostoyevsky, but I guess it’s more accessible, and besides, it’s still an excellent novel when compared to all literature. As usual for Dostoyevsky, his characters’ minds are very complex; I found it very interesting to see the way he cycled through pride, spite, looking for escapism, and mental breakdowns while taunting people for being unaware he got away with a crime, and underneath it all, yearning for compassion. Mr. Marmeladov I also find interesting — his philosophies of punishing yet pitying the guilty and suffering to gain empathy for others really show Dostoyevsky's remarkable ability to understand very specific but realistic psychological states.

There are some passages that I still remember about this book after reading it over a year ago; most notably, “suffering and pain are always obligatory for a broad consciousness and a deep heart.” It sounds pretty edgy, but it’s completely true. As a pianist (and therefore an artist), I try my best to constantly seek new thoughts and sensations — even painful or dangerous ones — in order to deepen my art, which is my life. And in general, the quote provides a good way to find meaning in life’s endless struggles.

Crime and Punishment (Dostoyevsky)

The book is about a Russian émigré who teaches his language in an American college, and who has a lot of trouble assimilating into American culture. Gogol once said that “the longer and more carefully we look at a funny story, the sadder it becomes,” which I think fits this book perfectly. It alternates between humor and gloom, between Pnin’s awkwardness and his deep loneliness; between poking fun at his confusion and abysmal English, and revealing his terrible memories of fleeing Russia because of the Revolution. Nabokov seems to have written it to make light of his own struggles to fit in when he first moved to the U.S.

It is largely humor and commentary, and as the child of immigrants who speaks Nabokov's languages, I can say the book is very relatable for people who are like me. The book is filled with Easter eggs for Russian speakers, as Nabokov loves to play with languages. A lot of Pnin's mistakes in English are clearly made by a Russian speaker, and numerous Russian phrases are thrown around (with translations.) And although my parents moved from Vietnam, a lot of Nabokov's experiences echoed my family's — having children unable to speak the language, dressing very impractically, seeking others of the same origins, and and being involved, though remotely, in the affairs of the old country.

Pnin (Nabokov)

Although mindsets ultimately influencing happiness is a well-known truth, this novella executes it very well. As proven in the first chapter, he never got anybody to truly care about him by the time he died, and, judging from the garbled nature of his final words, they might not have even known anything had changed about him. Life continued on around him as usual, but it didn't matter. The change within him, pushing him to simply love everyone despite everything, and to stop fearing his fate, was enough to bring him final peace.

The book has a thematic ambiguity that sort of reminds me of Moby-Dick — although it's primarily about how Ivan dealt with his inescapable death, Tolstoy also weaves in themes of reconciling with a shallow, wasted life, and accepting God rather than struggling against and ignoring His presence. In general, the book seems to relate to facing everything that is inevitable and profound in life. Things that make all that you've previously experienced feel trivial or pointless in comparison, and pose problems too difficult for other people to understand or help you through. You have to deal with them on your own, and the more you try to ignore them, the worse they become.
